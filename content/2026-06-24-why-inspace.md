### Childhood Curiosity
As a kid, I would often break things apart. One of my favourite things in the world was a cassette player. I just couldn't wait to set my eyes on one. I'm sure you can guess the component I'm that I'm talking about. 

Let me drop a hint. Once I had this component and a battery, I could make it spin. I'm not exactly sure who taught me this, but the moment I knew it was possible, I couldn't wait to find more bad players — [whispering] (sometimes it didn't have to be bad 😉).

> Yes, It's a capstan motor speed servo device (basically the component that spins the cassete film). One of my uncle is an Electronics Engineer and he had plenty of damaged cassete players lying around.

I didn't understand math, physics, general electronics, or computer science then. Of course, I was a kid. 

Just having fun playing with an Servo component made me happy. 

One day my curiosity led me to explore the Servo component further. I saw a few thin cooper writing — as a kid i didn't know what I was looking at. Sadly, it got damaged in the process. But I learned something important that day.

> Experiments come at a price if you're willing to trade and learn from making mistakes

But that didn't stop me from hacking my way around and trying to understand how it worked underneath. Besides, I knew I could get another cassette if I searched harder in my uncle's junk box. 

And I had many more hacks:
It wasn't just the Servo from the cassette player. 
It wasn't just the speaker from a radio.
It wasn't just the light bulb from the huge aluminium handheld torch from the 1990s. 
It wasn't just a cable antenna from an old (black & white) TV.

It was anything that I couldn't quite understand. I often wondered how many things could be powered by electricity. I wanted to _explore_ it all because it felt awesome.

### Early Tech Adventures

I would say I had a fun childhood. I had the opportunity to watch Ultimate Mortal on the SEGA console while my "brodas" played until I became old enough to play myself. I played the GameBoy Nintendo, the GameBoy Advance, and PS1, PS2, with my favorite game being DMC (Devil May Cry). I'm sure you're wondering why all of this is relevant. Just hang on; I'm also there. At that point I believed one can run a game on any digital device and made it my life's work to find a game for every device. It just had to be possible. 

> I got a story of how I was ban from a Cybercafe because I made it GameHub after install SEGA games on their computers for my street mates. Fun times.

Fast-forward to 2007, when I had access to my other uncle's computer. It was one of the now ancient hefty ones for that matter. It was a Fujitsu laptop — Yes, Fujitsu.

Most kids my age didn't have access to a personal laptop computer at that age, but I did, and I was 11 years old.
Little did I know, my life was about to change. This was where my creative journey started. I learned how to operate 
the Windows XP OS and a few Microsoft Office Suite softwares — even though I was completely clueless about what I was doing, it was fun.
I got into every possible user interface of this operating system, trying to understand how things worked. __Learning by doing__ and 
this was the early stages of the internet in Nigerian, but we didn't have EDGE in my region yet.

Eventually, the internet became a thing. I downloaded all kinds of games from Wapking, Waptrick, and Sefan.ru for Nokia Java phones.
I joined 2go and got to experience virtual communication and interactions with peers without having to spend time in a cybercafe.
I joined Facebook, started learning web design (HTML & CSS) after an introductory course in a church program — not sure where I would be
if not for that bootcamp.

I wanted to be like Mark Zuckerberg at some point. You see I had just learned about him, and I wanted to know
how to create my own social platform like facebook.com — Ah!!! Wishful thinking. The Bible says in "1 Corinthians 13:11 — When I was a child, I talked like a child..." I'll just leave it there. "A poor Nigerian boy becomes a successful technology CEO" — wouldn't that be nice for the history books? Sadly, that's not how life works. More on this later...

Do I regret starting that journey? Absolutely not!
I'm actually super happy that I did, 100 percent, regardless of the challenges I've had to experience along the way.
I'll write about my experience in the university some other time.

### What's all this talk about?

In all of my experience — from my humble, to arrogant, and back to humble stage — I've realized that absolutely NO ONE can do it on their own.
Read "Outliers" by Malcolm Gladwell if you believe otherwise. You see I grow up thinking that I could do it all. I could be everything and anything I wanted to be. That gave me the determination I needed to explore. Once I meet someone as curious and crazier — Bradley Yarrow. I understood the blessing of finally finding a person who gets it. Now imagine how crazier things get when you find 3 more people. That's what the InSpace community is about. __A space for nerds who get it.__. If you're not — I'm sorry, you're not invited.

> You can be a nerd at anything. You only have to be obsessed about the details that normies overlook.

### Who is the InSpace community for?
I have been in the Port Harcourt technology ecosystem for well over a decade. I have taught, attended and spoken in both major and minor technology events/programs like TechCreek, GDG Port Harcourt etc, just so you get a better picture of how involved I've been. Every time I attend these events, it feels mediocre in my opinion. From the very first day I've always felt like we talk too much.

> Introduction to LLM, Introduction to Firebase, Introduction.., Introduction..., Introduction...

Well, I'm tired of talking too much. If you care enough to level up your experience, to build and do work that matters, to show up every week, to make mistakes and to learn from them, to resonate with like minds, to explore, experiment and hack with a group of nerds. If you're ready to act and shut the noise. Come with your ideas as we can never have enough.

A community of people of Makers await you. And don't worry, we have all the resources to keep your curiosity burning.
