As a kid, I would often break things apart. I was curious long before I understood the meaning of Curiousity. 
I just couldn't way to set my eyes on a cassete player. 

To me then i only had one purpose. Which could be fulfilled without a battery. 
The point of destroying the cassete you see as to get a particular kind of component. 
If you were like me I'm sure you can guess the component I'm talking about. 

Yes! it's an oscillator device. Most cassete players come with one and lucky my uncle is
an electronics engineer (so I had plenty to destroy). That's besides the point. 

Once I had this component and a battery I could make this component spin. I'm not exactly sure who taugh me this 
but the moment I knew that was possible. I couldn't wait to set my eyes on a bad cassete — [whispering] (sometimes it doesn't even have to be bad. 😉).
I didn't understand maths, physics, general electronics or computer science then. Of course I was a kid. 
Just having fun playing with an oscilatory component that makes me happy. 

One day my curiously led me to opening exploring this components of this component. Sadly, it got bad in the process. But I learn something new that day.

> Experiments come at a price. Exploration is sometimes expensive. 

But that didn't stop me from hacking my way around and trying to understand how it worked underneath.
Besides, I knew I could get another cassete if I search harded in my uncle's junk box. 

It wasn't just the oscillatory device from the cassete. 
It wasn't just the speaker from a radio.
It wasn't just a cable antenna from a old (black & white tele).
It wasn't just the ligh bulb from the huge hand held torch from the 1990's 
It was anything that I couldn't quite understand. I find my wondering how many things could be powered by electricity.
And I wanted to explore all of them because It was amazing to me. 

I would say I had a fun childhood. I had the opportunity to watch the Ultimate Mortal in the SEGA console while my Broda's played until I became of age and learn how to play myself. I played the GameBoy Ninetendo, the GameBoy Advance, and PS1, PS2 with my favourite game being DMC (Devil May Cry). I'm sure you're wondering why all of this is relevant. Just hang on a little bit I'm also there.

Fast-forward to 2007, when I had access to my other uncle's computer. A Fujistu computer — Yes, Fujistu. 
It was a laptop to be precise, one of the now ancient hefty ones.

Most kids my age didn't have access to a personal laptop computer at that age but I did and I was 11 years old.
Little did I know my life was about to change. This was where my creative journey started. I learnt how to operate 
the Windows XP OS and a few Microsoft Office Suite softwares — even though I was completely clueless of what I was doing.
I got into every possible UI of this operating system trying to understand how things worked. _Learning by doing_ and 
there was no internet then, not even EDGE.

Eventually, the internet became a thing. I downloaded all kinds of games from wapking, waptrick, sefan.ru for Nokia phones.
I joined 2go and got to experience virtual communication and interactions with peers without having to spend time in a cybercafe.
I joined facebook, started learning web design (HTML & CSS) after an introductory course in a church program — Not sure what where I would be
if not for that bootcamp.

Actually,  I wanted to be like Mark Zuckerburg that's why I started learning web design. You see, I had just learnt of him and I wanted to know
how to create Facebook.com — Ah!!! wishful thinking. The bible says in "1 Corinthians 13:11 — When I was a child, I talked like a child..." I'll just leave it there. A poor Nigerian boy with very limited resources become a successful technology CEO. Woudn't that be nice one for the History books. Sadly, that's no how
life works. More on this later...

Do I regret starting that journey? Absolutely Not!
I'm actually super happy that I did 100 percent regardless of the challenges a long the way.

I'll talk about my experience in the University experience in another time.

Now, for the mean reason why I started this blog: "InSpace".

In all my of my experience. From my humble, to arrogant and back to humble stage. I've realized that absolutely NO ONE. Can do it on their own.
Read "Outliers" by Malcolm Gladwell if you believe otherwise. 

## The Problem
I have been in the Port-Harcourt technology ecosystem for a well over a decade. I have attended and spoken in a few events just so you get a better picture
of how involved I've been. Every time I attend these events it reminds me a *Nigerian Public University Classroom* with an outdated and some worth useless
curricullum designed for novices — which is totally fine. I'm sure some people were inspired to take their technology journey seriously from these events.
Interestingly, i don't fall in that category. 

I felt like nothing was changing and there was nothing I could do regardless of my efforts to influence the leadership of these communities to take
things up a knot, to drive real conversations, to support builders, to make knowledge sharing a core part of the community value.
