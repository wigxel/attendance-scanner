### Childhood Curiosity
As a kid, I had a habit of taking things apart.
One of the things that fascinated me the most was the cassette player. I could not wait to get my hands on one, and it had nothing to do with listening to music. There was one tiny component inside that completely captured my attention. If I took it out and connected it to a battery, it would spin. I still cannot remember who showed me that trick, but the moment I saw it happen, I was hooked. From then on, I was always looking for broken cassette players. Whispering… sometimes they did not even have to be broken. 😉

The part I was obsessed with was the capstan motor, the little motor that spins the cassette tape. 

One of my uncles was an electronics engineer, so there was never a shortage of damaged cassette players lying around his workshop. At the time, I knew nothing about mathematics, physics, electronics, or computer science. I was just a kid. And somehow, playing with that tiny motor made me ridiculously happy.

One day, curiosity got the better of me. I wanted to see what was inside the motor, so I carefully took it apart. I found these thin copper windings, although I had no idea what they were. By the time I was done investigating, I had damaged the motor. That was the day I learned one of my first lessons about building things.

> Experiments come at a price. If you are willing to learn, you have to be willing to make mistakes.

That lesson did not stop me. If anything, it made me even more curious. I knew I could always find another cassette player if I searched hard enough through my uncle’s junk box. Soon, it was no longer just cassette players. I took apart radio speakers, the light bulb from those huge aluminium torches that almost every home seemed to have in the 1990s, cable antennas from old black and white televisions, and anything else I could not quite understand. If it ran on electricity, I wanted to know how it worked.
I wanted to understand all of it.

### Early Tech Adventures
Looking back, I had a really fun childhood.
Before I was old enough to play, I spent hours watching my “brodas” play Ultimate Mortal Kombat on the SEGA console. Then came the Nintendo Game Boy, the Game Boy Advance, PlayStation 1, and PlayStation 2. My favourite game was Devil May Cry. You’re probably wondering what all of this has to do with InSpace. Just hang on. I am also getting there.
Somewhere along the line, I became convinced that if a device had a screen, someone had probably figured out how to run a game on it. I made it my personal mission to find games for every digital device I could get my hands on. It just had to be possible.

There is a funny story about how I got banned from a cybercafé because I installed SEGA games on several of their computers. Before long, my street mates had turned the place into our own little gaming hub.

Fun times.
Then came 2007. I got access to another one of my uncle’s computers. It was one of those huge laptops that would probably qualify as antiques today. A Fujitsu laptop. Yes, Fujitsu. I was only 11 years old. Most kids my age did not have access to a personal computer, but I did. Looking back, I had no idea that laptop was about to change my life.

That was where my creative journey really began. I learned how to use Windows XP and Microsoft Office by doing what I knew best, clicking everything I could find. Every menu, every setting, every window was something new to explore. Most of the time I had no idea what I was doing, but that never stopped me.

Those were the early days of the internet in Nigeria. EDGE had not reached my part of the country yet, so getting online was still a luxury. When internet access became more common, I jumped right in. I downloaded Java games for Nokia phones from WapKing, Waptrick, and Sefan.ru. I joined 2go and experienced online conversations without having to visit a cybercafé. Then came Facebook.

Around that same time, I attended a church programme where someone introduced us to HTML and CSS. Looking back now, I honestly do not know where I would be if I had not attended that programme.

For a while, I wanted to be like Mark Zuckerberg. I had just learned about him, and suddenly I was convinced I could build my own version of Facebook. Ah!!! Wishful thinking. The Bible says in 1 Corinthians 13:11, “When I was a child, I talked like a child…” I will just leave that there.

The idea of a poor Nigerian boy growing up to build one of the world’s biggest technology companies sounded like the perfect story. Life, however, had other plans.

More on that later.

Do I regret starting that journey? Not for a second. It has not been an easy road, but I would choose it all over again. Every challenge, every mistake, every lesson has shaped the person I am today.

The university years deserve their own story, and I will save that for another day.

So, What Is All This About?
Looking back on my journey, from being humble, to becoming a little arrogant, and finding my way back to being humble again, one lesson stands above everything else.

Nobody builds anything meaningful alone. If you believe otherwise, read Outliers by Malcolm Gladwell.

For a long time, I believed I could do everything by myself. I thought I could become anything I wanted if I worked hard enough. That belief gave me the confidence to explore, experiment, and keep learning. It also made me believe I did not need anyone else.

Then I met Bradley Yarrow.

For the first time, I met someone whose curiosity matched mine. Someone who enjoyed figuring things out simply because it was fun. I finally understood the blessing of finding someone who simply gets it. Now imagine what happens when you find three more people like that.

That is what the InSpace community is about. A space for nerds who get it.

You can be a nerd about anything. Technology. Engineering. Design. Business. Writing. Music. Art. It does not matter. What matters is being obsessed with the details that normies overlook. If you are not one of those people, I am sorry, you are not invited.

### Who Is the InSpace Community For?
I have been part of the Port Harcourt technology ecosystem for well over a decade. I have attended events, taught at events, spoken at events, and volunteered at events. Communities and Companies like TechCreek, Olotu Square, Google Developer Groups Port Harcourt, and many others have contributed to my journey. I appreciate what they do. I genuinely do. But I have always felt something was missing. We spend too much time talking.

> Introduction to LLMs.  
Introduction to Firebase.  
Introduction to…  
Introduction to…  
Introduction to…  

At some point, we have to stop collecting introductions and start building.

That is why InSpace exists.
